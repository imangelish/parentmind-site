EmotionsAge 3–5
Meltdown in a public place — nothing works
"He screamed for 20 minutes in the supermarket. People were staring."
Why this happens
At 3–5, the prefrontal cortex is still largely undeveloped. When a child hears "no," the emotional brain floods before any rational processing can happen. This is a neurological event, not defiance.
What to do
1

Do not reason or negotiate. Get low — crouch to their level.

2

Name the feeling: "You really wanted that. You're so disappointed."

3

Create safety, not shame. Move somewhere quieter if possible.

Watch for
Meltdowns lasting 30+ min or daily — flag for pediatric assessment.
BehaviorAge 4–7
Refuses kindergarten — cries every morning
"She holds onto the door. Teacher says she's fine 10 minutes after I leave."
Why this happens
Separation anxiety is developmentally normal. The stomach ache is real — anxiety activates the gut-brain axis. Settling quickly after you leave is a good sign: she has a secure base.
What to do
1

Create a consistent goodbye ritual — same words, same hug, every day.

2

Do not linger. A long goodbye signals something is wrong.

3

Give her something of yours to hold during the day.

Watch for
Escalating avoidance after 4+ weeks of consistent routine.
SleepAge 2–6
Won't sleep alone — needs a parent in the room
"He's 5 and still can't fall asleep unless I'm sitting next to him."
Why this happens
Sleep is neurologically the most vulnerable state for a child. His nervous system needs co-regulation — a calm adult presence to downshift. This is not manipulation.
What to do
1

Gradual distancing: next to bed → doorway → outside. Move one step every 3–5 nights.

2

Introduce 10-min "worry time" before bed — named fears sleep better.

3

Give him agency: choose nightlight color, blanket. Control reduces anxiety.

Watch for
Night terrors (screaming but unresponsive) have a separate protocol — flag separately.
Self-esteemAge 6–10
"I hate myself" — said after one wrong answer
"My son got one answer wrong and said 'I'm so stupid, I hate myself.'"
Why this happens
School-age children are in Erikson's Industry vs. Inferiority stage — testing "am I competent?" A failure can feel existential. "I hate myself" is catastrophizing but deserves a serious response.
What to do
1

Don't immediately correct. First: "That feels really awful."

2

Separate event from identity: "Getting one wrong doesn't say anything about who you are."

3

Share your own failure story — normalizing struggle is one of the most evidence-based resilience builders.

Watch for
Frequent self-critical language or withdrawal from activities — consider a school counselor.
BehaviorAge 2–5
Hits other children — especially at daycare
"My daughter hit another child twice this week. We don't hit at home."
Why this happens
Before age 5, hitting is almost always a language limitation, not aggression. The child has a feeling they cannot verbalize fast enough. Daycare amplifies this — high stimulation, low control.
What to do
1

Respond calmly: "Hitting hurts. When you're angry, use your words."

2

Practice missing words at home during calm moments — give her vocabulary before she needs it.

3

Notice the pattern — before lunch? At pickup? Hunger and fatigue amplify this significantly.

Watch for
If hitting is accompanied by biting or seems to give visible pleasure — warrants a closer look.
BoundariesAge 3–6
Extreme reaction to "no" — every single time
"I've started avoiding saying no just to have peace."
Why this happens
Some children have a lower frustration tolerance threshold — partly temperament, partly developmental. Avoiding "no" teaches that intensity = outcome, which can entrench the behavior.
What to do
1

Reintroduce low-stakes "nos" consistently. Build her tolerance in small doses.

2

Offer "yes, later" when possible — validates desire while holding the boundary.

3

After the storm: "What could you do next time when that feeling comes?"

Watch for
Consistent parental avoidance of limit-setting is worth exploring with a family therapist.

About ParentMind

Why this exists

I'm a pedagogue-psychologist by training, specializing in child psychology and early childhood education. I work directly with children through regular supervised placements — not in theory, but in the moment when a meltdown has already started. The longer I do this, the more clearly I see that the hardest part isn't the children. It's the parents left alone with it. I'm not a mother myself, and I don't want to pretend I know what that costs. ParentMind exists to share the part of the weight psychology can explain — not to replace what only you can carry.

— Founder, ParentMind
